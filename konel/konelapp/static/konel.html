Ancient philosophy refers to the era of thought originating in ancient Greece around the 6th century BCE and persisting until the fall of the Roman Empire (roughly the 5th and 6th century CE). It is considered the birthplace of Western culture, distinguished by its shift from mythological explanations to naturalistic and reasoned discourse about the cosmos, ethics, and human existence. Key figures and schools define this tradition, beginning with the Pre-Socratics like Thales, Anaximander, and Heraclitus, who sought natural causes for phenomena. This was followed by the Classical trio of Socrates, Plato, and Aristotle, whose work laid the foundations for logic, ethics, and metaphysics. Later, Hellenistic schools such as Stoicism, Epicureanism, Cynicism, and Platonism further developed these ideas, emphasizing ethics, the nature of the good life, and the relationship between the individual and the universe.
